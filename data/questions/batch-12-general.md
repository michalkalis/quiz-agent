**Q:** The phrase "the exception that proves the rule" is used constantly, but almost always in a way that makes no logical sense. When people say it, they typically mean that a counter-example somehow confirms or validates the rule — which is backwards. Exceptions weaken rules; they don't strengthen them. What does the phrase actually mean, and what is the logic behind the original sense?  
**A:** The phrase comes from the Latin legal maxim "exceptio probat regulam in casibus non exceptis" — "the exception proves the rule in cases not excepted." In its original legal context, "proves" carried the older meaning of "tests" or "demonstrates the existence of" (from Latin "probare," which has both senses), not "confirms." The logic is: if you need to explicitly state that a rule does not apply in a specific case, the very existence of that exception implies the rule holds everywhere else. A sign reading "No parking on Sundays" proves — in the original sense of demonstrating — that a general rule permitting parking exists on the other days. Without a general rule of permitted parking, there would be no need to carve out an exception. So the phrase doesn't mean "an anomaly confirms the general pattern." It means: the need to state an exception implies a general rule exists for all other cases. The modern usage — treating a counter-example as validation — is the precise opposite of what the phrase means.  
**Alt answers:** "proves" originally meant "tests" or "demonstrates the existence of" — the logic is: if you must state an exception to a rule, the rule implicitly applies everywhere else; a "No Parking on Sundays" sign proves parking is permitted other days
