**A:** The Eiffel Tower grows approximately 15 to 18 centimetres taller in summer than in winter. Iron expands when heated — a property called thermal expansion — at a rate of about 11 parts per million per degree Celsius. When the tower's iron structure heats up in the Paris summer sun (the steel can reach 40°C or more), the metal expands enough to push the structure upward by roughly a sixth of a metre. In winter, the iron contracts and the tower returns to its cooler, shorter height. The tower can also lean slightly away from the sun on one side — by up to 18 centimetres — when the sun heats one face more than the other. The heated face expands fractionally more, pushing the tip away from the heated side. This is a fully reversible, elastic deformation and causes no structural damage. Gustave Eiffel anticipated thermal expansion in his calculations and the open lattice design dissipates heat quickly, reducing the effect. The same physics governs all large metal bridges — expansion joints exist specifically to allow bridges to change length with temperature without buckling.

**A:** In the early 19th century, golfers noticed that old, beat-up, scuffed balls consistently flew further than smooth new ones. The original "featherie" ball — a leather pouch stuffed with wet goose feathers — would develop a rough, pocked surface with use. Golfers began deliberately roughing up new balls to improve their distance. When the gutta-percha ball replaced the featherie in the 1840s–1850s, manufacturers discovered the same effect: battered old gutta-percha balls flew further than pristine ones. By the 1890s, manufacturers were deliberately casting balls with raised bumps; by the early 20th century, these had evolved into the recessed dimples used today. The physics explains why: a smooth ball in flight creates a large area of separated low-pressure air behind it (a drag-inducing "wake"). Dimples cause the thin layer of air around the ball to stay attached further around the ball's surface before separating, drastically reducing the wake and the drag — by as much as 50%. Dimples also interact with the ball's backspin to generate aerodynamic lift through the Magnus effect, helping it stay airborne longer. A dimpled golf ball can travel roughly twice as far as a smooth ball hit with the same force.

Honey is one of the very few foods that can last indefinitely without artificial preservation. Several reinforcing properties make this possible. First, honey has extremely low water content — typically 17 to 20% — which denies bacteria and yeasts the moisture they need to survive and reproduce. Second, honey is naturally acidic, with a pH of 3.5 to 4.5, which inhibits most microorganisms. Third, during production, bees add an enzyme called glucose oxidase; when honey absorbs even trace moisture, this enzyme produces small amounts of hydrogen peroxide — a natural antibacterial agent. Fourth, honey's extremely high sugar concentration creates osmotic pressure that pulls water out of any bacterial cells that enter it, killing them through dehydration. Together, these four mechanisms make honey genuinely self-preserving. If properly sealed against moisture, there is no known upper limit to how long honey can remain edible.
