**Q:** In 1999, psychologists David Dunning and Justin Kruger published a paper based on four studies at Cornell University, testing whether people who lacked competence in a given area also lacked the metacognitive ability to recognise their own incompetence. Their findings became perhaps the most widely cited result in modern popular psychology, routinely invoked to dismiss people in political arguments, comment sections, and management literature. What the studies actually found, however, was both more nuanced and less flattering to the people who invoke the finding most confidently. What was the real finding, and who did the studies suggest was most likely to misjudge their own ability?  
**A:** The original Dunning-Kruger studies found that low-performing subjects in tests of logical reasoning, grammar, and humour consistently overestimated their performance — scoring in roughly the 12th percentile but believing they had performed around the 62nd. This is real and robust. What is often omitted is the finding in the opposite direction: high-performing subjects systematically *underestimated* how well they had done, assuming others had performed similarly to themselves. Both failures were forms of miscalibration, with opposite directions. The popular version of the "Dunning-Kruger effect" — a simple U-shaped curve where idiots are supremely confident and experts are appropriately humble — is a significant simplification of the data. Subsequent research has added further complexity: some replication attempts have found the pattern to be partly a statistical artefact (regression to the mean), and the effect size varies considerably across domains and cultures. Dunning and Kruger themselves have noted that their paper is regularly cited to dismiss people in internet arguments, and that using it this way — confidently applying it to others while exempting yourself — is itself a clean demonstration of the metacognitive failure the paper describes.
